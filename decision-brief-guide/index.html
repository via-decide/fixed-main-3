Build a Policy Brief in Under 5 Minutes

ViaDecide Team 12 June 2025 4 min read

A well-structured policy brief used to require hours of research, a team of analysts, and an expensive subscription to a think-tank database. ViaDecide compresses that workflow to under five minutes using open data, structured synthesis, and a clean export.

This is a complete walkthrough — from blank input to a sharable, exportable PDF brief.

Who this is for: Students writing policy essays, founders evaluating a regulatory landscape, local officials drafting a recommendation, researchers needing a quick literature anchor before going deep. The output is a starting point, not a final document — but it's a very good starting point.

Step-by-step guide

1
Enter your research topic

Open the Student Research Tool. Type your topic — be specific. "Water privatization in Maharashtra" will return better results than "water". The more specific your query, the more targeted the synthesis.

2
Review the four-source output

The engine queries Wikipedia, Wikivoyage, Wikibooks, and Wikinews simultaneously. Review each source's contribution — you'll see what each adds. Encyclopedia context, regional data, procedural info, and recent news.

3
Check the synthesis panel

The synthesis tab shows a structured summary: primary thesis, supporting evidence, contradictions between sources, and identified knowledge gaps. This is your brief skeleton.

4
Open Decision Brief (PDF)

Navigate to the Decision Brief tool. Your research session's synthesis is available to import. Add your own framing, context, and recommendations in the editable fields.

5
Export and share

Download as a structured PDF or copy the shareable link. The brief includes a timestamp, source list, and your affiliate tag for tracking. It's policy-grade and citation-ready.

✦ Pro tip

Run two searches: one broad (e.g., "ONDC network") and one specific (e.g., "ONDC agriculture small farmers 2024"). Combine the synthesis from both to get encyclopedic context plus current-state granularity in the same brief.

What makes a good research query

The engine works best with noun phrases, not questions. "Crop insurance scheme PM-FASAL" will outperform "how does crop insurance work in India?". Think of it like a Wikipedia search, not a Google search — you want to name the topic, not describe your curiosity about it.

For policy topics, include the domain and geography: "urban planning regulations Bengaluru", "healthcare privatization Tamil Nadu", "water allocation Cauvery basin". The geographic specificity unlocks Wikivoyage's regional data, which is often the most valuable source for location-dependent policy questions.

Limitations to know about

The four Wikimedia sources are excellent for established topics with significant public coverage. For highly niche, very recent, or technical regulatory subjects, coverage may be thin. In those cases, use ViaDecide's synthesis as a background anchor and layer in primary sources — government gazettes, official reports, court judgments — manually.

The engine does not connect to paywalled sources, internal government databases, or proprietary datasets. Everything it synthesizes is openly licensed and verifiable. That's a strength for transparency and a constraint for very specialist domains.
