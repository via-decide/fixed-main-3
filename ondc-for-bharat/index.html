ONDC: Open Commerce for Every Indian

ViaDecide Research 10 July 2025 5 min read

India's e-commerce market is dominated by two or three large platforms. Small sellers — the kirana around the corner, the weaver in Pochampally, the pharmacist in Patna — largely play by someone else's rules, inside someone else's walled garden, paying someone else's commission.

ONDC changes that equation fundamentally. The Open Network for Digital Commerce is not a marketplace. It's a protocol — like how UPI is not a bank but an open payments rail. ONDC is an open commerce rail.

One line summary: ONDC separates the buyer app from the seller app — meaning a customer on Paytm can buy from a seller listed on Meesho, without either platform controlling the transaction flow. Open. Interoperable. Bharat-scale.

How ONDC actually works

01
Buyer App Any ONDC-registered buyer app (Paytm, Magicpin, etc.) where the consumer discovers products.
02
ONDC Gateway The protocol layer that broadcasts the buyer's intent to all registered seller apps on the network simultaneously.
03
Seller App Any ONDC-registered seller app responds with catalog, pricing, and fulfillment — regardless of which buyer app the consumer uses.
04
Logistics Provider Delivery is handled by whichever logistics network (Dunzo, Shiprocket, etc.) is selected — decoupled from both buyer and seller apps.

Why this matters for small sellers

On a traditional platform like Amazon or Flipkart, a seller is locked into that platform's discovery algorithm, commission structure, returns policy, and logistics. Visibility is pay-to-play. A small kirana in Nashik competes with a mega-warehouse in Bhiwandi on terms set entirely by the platform.

On ONDC, a seller on any registered app is discoverable from any buyer app. The commission structure is negotiated at the app level, not dictated by a monopoly gateway. Logistics is pluggable. And critically — the seller's customer relationship data stays with the seller, not the platform.

Current scale and what's coming

As of mid-2025, ONDC has crossed 10 million monthly transactions across food, grocery, mobility, and financial services. The network has onboarded over 6 lakh sellers — predominantly small merchants who were previously un-served or underserved by traditional e-commerce platforms.

The next wave: ONDC for agriculture (kisan-to-consumer direct trade), ONDC for health (pharmacy + diagnostics), and ONDC for credit (embedded lending at point-of-commerce). Each of these represents a multi-billion dollar market previously gated by platform dependencies.
